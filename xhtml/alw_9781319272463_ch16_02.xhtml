16.1 The Two-Way ANOVA Model

When you complete this section, you will be able to:

  • Discuss the advantages of a two-way ANOVA.
  • Describe the two-way ANOVA model and when it is used for inference.
  • Interpret the relationship between two factors in terms of main effects and interaction.
  • Construct an interaction plot and determine whether it shows that there is interaction between the factors.

We begin with a discussion of the advantages of the two-way ANOVA design and illustrate these with some examples. Then, we discuss the two-way ANOVA model and the breakdown of population means into main effects and interaction.

Advantages of two-way ANOVA

level of a factor, p. 144

In one-way ANOVA, we classify populations according to one categorical variable, or factor. In the two-way ANOVA model, there are two factors, each with several levels. When we are interested in the effects of two factors, a two-way design offers great advantages over two single-factor studies.

Example 16.1

Experimental Design 1: Best Produce Display and Signage

In Example 9.1 (page 458), a grocery store chain wants to compare three different produce displays. To do this, the chain randomly assigns 20 stores to each of the three displays. The amount of produce sold during a one-week period is the outcome variable.

Now suppose a second experiment is planned for the following week to compare four different styles of produce signage (e.g., a large sign with raised lettering or a hanging circular sign). A similar experimental design will be used, with the four styles randomly assigned equally among the same 60 stores.

Here is the design of the first experiment with the sample sizes:

Display n
1 20
2 20
3 20
Total 60

And here is the second experiment:

Style n
1 15
2 15
3 15
4 15
Total 60

In the first experiment, 20 stores were assigned to each level of the factor for a total of 60 stores. In the second experiment, 15 stores were assigned to each level of the factor for a total of 60 stores. Each experiment lasts one week, so the total amount of time for the two experiments is two weeks.

Each experiment will be analyzed using one-way ANOVA. The factor in the first experiment is Display with three levels, and the factor in the second experiment is Style with four levels. Let’s now consider combining the two experiments into one.

Example 16.2

Experimental Design 2: Best Produce Display and Signage

Suppose we use a two-way approach for the produce sales study. There are two factors, Display and Style. Because Display has three levels and Style has four levels, this is a 3×4 design. This gives a total of 12 possible combinations of Display and Style. With a total of 60 stores, we could assign each combination of produce display and signage style to five stores. The amount of produce sold during a one-week period is the outcome variable.

Here is a picture of the two-way design with the sample sizes:

  Style  
Display 1 2 3 4 Total
1 5 5 5 5 20
2 5 5 5 5 20
3 5 5 5 5 20
Total 15 15 15 15 60

Each combination of the factors in a two-way design corresponds to a cell. The 3×4 ANOVA for the produce experiment has 12 cells, each corresponding to a particular combination of Display and Style.

With the two-way design for Display and Style, notice that we have 20 stores assigned to each level of Display, the same as we had for the one-way experiment for Display alone. Similarly, there are 15 stores assigned to each level of Style. Thus, the two-way design gives us the same amount of information for estimating the amount of produce sold for each level of each factor as we had with the two one-way designs. The difference is that we can collect all the information in only one experiment. This experiment lasts one week (instead of a combined two weeks) and involves a single observation from each of the 60 stores. By combining the two factors into one experiment, we have increased our efficiency by reducing the amount of data to be collected by half.

Example 16.3

The Effect of a Limited Time Offer on Purchase Intent

Starbucks’ Pumpkin Spice Latte (PSL) is the company’s most popular seasonal item. Why is this? Is it the unique flavor? Or could it be because it is available for only a limited time each year?

To investigate this, some students surveyed 100 Starbucks consumers about their intent to purchase a PSL when it is offered in the fall.3 Half of the surveys included the upcoming PSL advertisement. The other half included the same advertisement with the additional words “Limited Time Offer” above the image of the drink.

Because purchase intent may depend on how frequently a consumer visits Starbucks, the students included a survey question about this. The question was used to classify each customer as either a “light” or “heavy” consumer of Starbucks. The students then analyzed these data using a two-way ANOVA.

Example 16.4

Two-Way ANOVA for the Purchase Intent Study

The factors for this two-way ANOVA are advertisement type with two levels and consumer status with two levels. There are 2×2=4 cells in their study. The outcome variable purchase intent is measured on a 1 to 7 scale.

Here is a table of sample sizes that summarizes their design:

  Consumer status  
Advertisement Light Heavy Total
Regular 27 23 50
Added wording 19 31 50
Total 46 54 100

The students were not able to control the number of subjects in each cell of the study because they did not know consumer status until the survey was administered.

This example illustrates a second reason for using two-way ANOVA. Although the students are primarily interested in the effect of adding the words “Limited Time Offer” on purchase intent, they also included consumer status because they suspected that there might be an effect.

DATA = FIT + RESIDUAL, p. 464

Consider an alternative one-way design where we ignore consumer status. With this design, we will have the same number of customers at each of the ad type levels, so in this way, it is similar to this two-way design. However, suppose that there is, in fact, an effect of consumer status. In this case, the one-way ANOVA would assign this variation to the RESIDUAL (within groups) part of the model. In the two-way ANOVA, user status is included as a factor; therefore, this variation is included in the FIT part of the model. Whenever we can move variation from RESIDUAL to FIT, we reduce the σ of our model and increase the power of our tests.

Example 16.5

Disorganized Shelf Display and Product Availability

Despite a retailer’s best efforts, products displayed on shelves can become disorganized and scarce. Does this affect the purchase likelihood of a product? Researchers consider the impact of shelf display (organized or disorganized) and product quantity (low, medium, high) on the purchase likelihood of a snack food product.4

Suppose we wanted to compare an organized and disorganized shelf display across three different product quantity levels. Instead of three separate one-way ANOVAs (or two-sample t tests), one for each quantity level, we make product quantity a factor in our design.

interaction, p. 662

This example illustrates a third reason for using two-way designs. The difference between the two shelf displays may depend on the product quantity. We call this an interaction when it occurs. In contrast, the average values for the shelf display effect and the product quantity effect are represented as main effects. The two-way model represents FIT as the sum of a main effect for each of the two factors and an interaction. One-way designs that vary a single factor and hold other factors fixed cannot discover interactions. We discuss interactions more fully later in this section.

These examples illustrate several reasons two-way designs are preferable to one-way designs.

Advantages of two-way ANOVA

  1. It is more efficient to study two factors simultaneously rather than separately.
  2. We can reduce the residual variation in a model by including a second factor thought to influence the response.
  3. We can investigate interactions between factors.

These considerations also apply to study designs with more than two factors. The statistical analysis in these situations is called higher-way ANOVA. Although some details grow more complex, the most important ideas are already present in the two-way setting. Because of this, we are content to explore only the two-way case. The choice of the design for data production (sample or experiment) is fundamental to any statistical study. Factors and levels must be carefully selected by an individual or team that understands both the statistical models and the issues that the study will address.

The two-way ANOVA model

When discussing two-way models in general, we use the labels A and B for the two factors. For particular examples and when using statistical software, it is better to use names for these categorical variables that suggest their meaning. Thus, in Example 16.2, we would say that the factors are Display and Style. The numbers of levels of the factors are often used to describe the model. Again referring to Example 16.2, we would call this a 3×4 ANOVA. Similarly, Example 16.4 illustrates a 2×2 ANOVA. In general, Factor A will have I levels and Factor B will have J levels. Therefore, we call the general two-way problem an I×J ANOVA.

In a two-way design, every level of A appears in combination with every level of B, so that I×J groups are compared. The sample size for level i of Factor A and level j of Factor B is nij.5 The total number of observations is

N=∑nij

The two-way ANOVA model

Consider that we have SRSs of size nij from each of I×J Normal populations. Let xijk represent the kth observation from the population having Factor A at level i and Factor B at level j. The two-way ANOVA model is

xijk=μij+εijk

for i=1,…, I and j=1,…, J and k=1,…, nij. The deviations εijk are from an N(0, σ) distribution. The parameters of the model are the means μij and the common standard deviation σ.

estimates of population parameters, p. 466

The FIT part of the model is the means μij, and the RESIDUAL part is the deviations εijk of the individual observations from their group means. To estimate a population mean μij, we use the sample mean of the observations from this group:

x¯ij=1nij∑kxijk

The k below the ∑ means that we sum the nij observations that belong to the (i, j)th group.

The residuals eijk=xijk−x¯ij reflect the variation about these sample means. They are again used in the calculations of the sample standard deviations as well as our assessment of whether the model conditions are approximately met.

rule for examining std devs, p. 467

The RESIDUAL part of the model contains the unknown σ. We calculate the sample variances for each SRS and, provided the rule of thumb for equal standard deviations is met, pool these to estimate σ2:

sp2=∑(nij−1)sij2∑(nij−1)

Just as in one-way ANOVA, the numerator in this fraction is SSE and the denominator is DFE. Also as in the one-way analysis, DFE is the total number of observations minus the number of groups. That is, DFE=N−IJ. The estimator of σ is sp, the pooled standard error.

Main effects and interactions

sums of squares, p. 471

Because we have independent samples from each of I×J groups, we can first think of the two-way ANOVA as a one-way ANOVA with IJ groups. Each population mean μij is estimated by the corresponding sample mean x¯ij, and we can calculate sums of squares and degrees of freedom as in one-way ANOVA. Thus, SSG is the group sum of squares constructed from deviations of the form x¯ij−x¯, where x¯ is the average of all the observations and x¯ij is the mean of the (i, j)th group. Similarly, DFG is simply IJ−1.

The first important distinction between one-way and two-way ANOVA is that in two-way ANOVA, we break down the FIT part of the model (the population means μij) in a way that reflects the presence of two factors. This means the terms SSG and DFG are broken down into terms corresponding to a main effect for A, a main effect for B, and an AB interaction. Each of SSG and DFG is then a sum of terms:

SSG=SSA+SSB+SSAB

and

DFG=DFA+DFB+DFAB

The term SSA represents variation among the means for the different levels of Factor A. Because there are I such means, DFA=I−1 degrees of freedom. Similarly, SSB represents variation among the means for the different levels of Factor B, with DFB=J−1.

Interactions are a bit more involved. We can see that SSAB, which is SSG−SSA−SSB, represents the variation in the group means that is not accounted for by the main effects. By subtraction, we see that its degrees of freedom are

DFAB=(IJ−1)−(I−1)−(J−1)=(I−1)(J−1)

There are many kinds of interactions. The easiest way to study them is through some examples.

Example 16.7

Per Capita Income

The American Community Survey provides annual per capita income for various subpopulations of the United States. Here are the per capita incomes for individuals of two ethnicities in two regions of the United States.6 (The data include people who earned nothing.)

Region White Asian Mean
Midwest $33,847 $34,554 $34,201
Northeast $41,456 $38,847 $40,152
Mean $37,652 $36,701 $37,176

The table also includes averages of the means in the rows and columns (rounded to the nearest dollar). For example, the second entry in the right margin is the average of the per capita income for individuals who claim to be of white or Asian descent in the Northeast:

41,456+38,8472=40,152

Similarly, the average per capita income of a white individual in the two regions is

33,847+41,4562=37,652

These averages are called marginal means because of their location at the margins of the table. The grand mean (37,176 in this case) can be obtained by averaging either set of marginal means or the four cell values.

It is clear from the marginal means that white individuals have a higher per capita income than Asian individuals and that individuals in the Northeast have a higher per capita income than those in the Midwest. These are main effects for the two factors. We can describe the main effects by the differences between the marginal means. On average, a person of white descent has a per capita income $951 higher than someone of Asian descent and a person in the Northeast has a per capita income that is $5951 more than an individual in the Midwest.

What about the interaction between region and ethnicity? An interaction is present if the main effects provide an incomplete description of the data. That is, if the ethnicity earnings gap is different in the two regions, then ethnicity and region interact. In this survey, the earnings gap is much larger in the Northeast:

  Midwest Northeast
White-Asian difference −$701 $2609

This difference in White-Asian differences suggests there is an interaction. Figure 16.1(a) is a plot of the four cell values. Because the difference between the per capita incomes is different in the two regions, the gap between the lines decreases from left to right. That is, the Midwest and Northeast per capita income lines are not parallel.

How would the plot look if there were no interaction? No interaction says that the regional earnings gap is the same in both ethnicities. That is, the effect of ethnicity does not depend on region. Suppose that the gap were $2609 in both regions. Figure 16.1(b) plots the means. The white and Asian lines are now parallel. Interaction between the factors is visible as lack of parallelism in a plot of the group means.

Plot a: The horizontal axis plots Ethnicity, with tick marks for White and Asian; and the vertical axis plots Per capita income (dollars), ranging from 33,000 to 42,000 in increments of 1,000. The line for Midwest extends from (White, approximately 34,000) to (Asian, approximately 34,800). The line for Northeast extends from (White, approximately 41,500) to (Asian, approximately 39,000).

Plot b: The horizontal axis plots Ethnicity, with tick marks for White and Asian; and the vertical axis plots Per capita income (dollars), ranging from 34,000 to 42,000 in increments of 1,000. The line for Midwest extends from (White, approximately 37,300) to (Asian, approximately 34,700). The line for Northeast extends from (White, approximately 41,500) to (Asian, approximately 39,000).

To examine two-way ANOVA data for a possible interaction, always construct a plot similar to Figure 16.1. Profiles that are roughly parallel imply that there is no clear interaction between the two factors. When no interaction is present, the marginal means provide a reasonable description of the two-way table of means.

In this case, it is clear that the two profiles (the collections of marginal means for a given region) are not parallel. When there is an interaction, the marginal means do not tell the whole story. For example, with these data, the marginal mean difference between regions is $5951. This is larger than the difference for Asian individuals ($4293) and smaller than the difference for white individuals ($7609).

As a result, the two lines are not parallel, indicating that an interaction is present. When there is interaction, main effects can be meaningful and important, but this is not always the case. For example, the main effect of region is meaningful despite the interaction, but the main effect of ethnicity is not as clear because of the interaction.

Example 16.9

Bundling as a Product Introduction Strategy

Bundling is a common marketing strategy that involves the sale of two or more separate products in one package. For example, a new cell phone might be bundled with a wireless service contract, or a new hair gel might be bundled with a popular body spray. Do the characteristics of the partnered product affect a consumer’s perception of the new product? A study was performed to investigate the effects of the partnered product’s brand image and complementarity on the new product’s perceived quality.7 The product’s quality is assessed through a series of 7-point scale questions, where a larger value refers to higher quality. The following table and Figure 16.3 summarize the group means.

  Complementarity  
Brand image Low High Total
Low 4.06 4.40 4.23
High 4.16 5.06 4.61
Total 4.11 4.73 4.42

The horizontal axis plots Complementarity, with tick marks for Low and High; and the vertical axis plots Average perceived quality, ranging from 4.0 to 5.2 in increments of 0.2. The line for Brand image: Low extends from (Low, approximately 4.06) to (High, approximately 4.37). The line for Brand image: High extends from (Low, approximately 4.18) to (High, approximately 5.08).

When the partnered product’s complementarity is low, perceived quality of the new product is about the same for the two levels of brand image. When the complementarity is high, the perceived quality increases regardless of brand image. However, the perceived quality increases more when the partnered product’s brand image is high than when it is low.

In a statistical analysis, the pattern of means shown in Figure 16.3 produced significant main effects for brand image and complementarity in addition to a brand image–by–complementarity interaction. The main effects record that perceived quality is higher when the partnered product has high brand image and when the partnered product complements the new product. This clearly does not tell the whole story. We need to discuss the complementarity effect in each of the brand image levels to fully understand how perceived quality is affected.

A different kind of interaction is present in the next example. Here, we must be very cautious in our interpretation of the main effects as one of them can lead to a distorted conclusion.

Example 16.10

Right Shade of Green

It is commonly thought that environmentally friendly consumption is driven by a desire to protect the environment, not to enhance one’s self-image. Is that the case in all settings? To study this, a two-factor experiment was run, in which subjects looked at an advertisement for a fuel-efficient vehicle and then reported their purchase intentions. One factor was whether the advertisement had a self-benefit or other-benefit appeal. The other factor was the type of accountability (public or private).8 For the private level, the subjects were told their responses would be confidential, while for the public level, the subjects were told their responses would be discussed in a small group. Here are the mean purchase intentions:

  Accountability  
Appeal Public Private Mean
Self-benefit 3.26 4.58 3.92
Other-benefit 4.84 3.37 4.11
Mean 4.05 3.98 4.01

The means are plotted in Figure 16.4. In the analysis of this experiment, only the interaction is statistically significant. How are we to interpret these results?

The horizontal axis plots Accountability, with tick marks for Private and Public; and the vertical axis plots Purchase intentions, ranging from 3.5 to 5.0 in increments of 0.5. The line for Appeal: Other-benefit extends from (Private, approximately 3.35) to (Public, approximately 4.80). What catches our eye in the plot is that the lines cross. The purchase intention is much higher for the self-benefit advertisement in the private accountability condition, but the purchase intention is much higher for the other-benefit advertisement in the public accountability condition. This interaction between accountability and benefit is the important result. The main effects for accountability and appeal are not practically meaningful because of this interaction. Both factors have effects, but we must know which type of accountability a person is under in order to say which type of appeal results in higher purchase intent.

Section 16.1 Summary

  • Two-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) is used to compare population means when populations are classified according to two factors.
  • Two-way ANOVA assumes that independent SRSs are drawn from each population and that the responses from each population are Normal with possibly different means, but the same standard deviation.
  • Marginal means are calculated by taking averages of the group means, when organized in a two-way table, either across rows or down columns. These means can be used in an interaction plot to aid in the interpretation of the results.
  • ANOVA separates the total variation into parts for the model and error. Pooling is used to estimate the error, or within-group variance. The model variation is separated into parts for each of the main effects and the interaction.
  • We describe main effects by the differences between marginal means. An interaction is present if the main effects provide an incomplete description of the group means. There are many different types of interaction so interaction plots are helpful aids.
